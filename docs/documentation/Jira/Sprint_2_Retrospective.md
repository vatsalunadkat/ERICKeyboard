# ERICK — Sprint 2 Retrospective

**Sprint**: SCRUM Sprint 2  
**Dates**: March 2–6, 2026 (Monday–Friday)
**Velocity**: ~95 story points planned  
**Sprint Goal**: Implement core keyboard on Android & iOS; begin game development in Godot  

---

## 👍 What Went Well

1. **Excellent task breakdown** — The Product Owner broke down large epic tasks into specific, actionable items that could be implemented individually. This enabled each member to deeply engage in the project. Task execution was operable and the overall progress rhythm was controllable.

2. **Good collaboration and communication atmosphere** — The team communicated smoothly and collaborated closely, sharing encountered difficulties and ideas openly.

3. **Significant learning and growth** — Most members completed the transition from getting familiar with tools to actually operating them during this sprint. They learned through exploration and documentation in the early stage and put knowledge into practice in the later stage. Professional abilities and project confidence both improved — all members reported learning a great deal.

4. **Strong task self-selection and adaptability** — Members chose tasks suited to their own abilities and preferences, leading to higher engagement and output quality.

---

## 👎 What Didn't Go Well

1. **Technical and equipment issues** — Some members' computer equipment failures affected development. Additionally, some members experienced confusion and logic issues during initial code integration, which hindered development progress.

2. **Insufficient progress control and visualization** — It was difficult for the team lead to judge actual development progress through daily stand-ups alone. Some members did not push their code to GitHub in a timely manner, making it impossible to track work progress through commit history.

3. **Logical connection issues between Android and iOS** — The business logic on the Android side had not been implemented in a timely manner, which partially prevented the iOS side from synchronously implementing related functions, creating a risk of duplicate work.
